The Read

After the ERP year, the integration work does not end. It shifts.

The City Beautiful centennial closed the way most of the city's IT year closed: with a stack of finished work. The INFOR CloudSuite cutover wrapped across Finance, HR, Supply Chain, Payroll, and Questica. The Smart City Hub added a year of open data and another Smart21 certification. The Ekin Spotter smart poles expanded from pilot to a ten-pole deployment. AIDA moved to a generative model. The Innovation and Technology Department shipped.

The next phase is harder than the last. The data contracts between INFOR, the ArcGIS Hub, the AWS-hosted Urban Analytics layer, and the Azure-hosted AIDA chatbot are where smart city programs quietly accumulate debt. A planner who wants a clean view of pedestrian counts, permit activity, and service requests against a street corridor ends up with three dashboards and a spreadsheet. A resident who asks AIDA about a zoning question gets a general answer because the chatbot does not read the permitting system. Over time, the glue code multiplies. Nobody owns it.

We read this as an orchestration problem, not a platform problem. Most municipal workflows break into three layers. Map the layers first and the governance story stays clean.

Teams that treat all three layers as one AI problem burn budget. Teams that map the layers first ship faster, and the governance story keeps pace with the build.

A Useful Frame

Three layers inside most municipal workflows

60%
Traditional code and database work Integration a competent engineering team can scope. ERP to GIS, GIS to citizen app, records to archive.
30%
Rule-based logic Permit routing, compliance checks, escalation paths. Decisions that want structured rules, not a language model.
10%
Generative AI, genuinely Summarizing long documents. Answering open citizen questions against a trusted knowledge base. Draft responses under human review.
